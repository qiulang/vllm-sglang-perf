### vLLM Performance Comparison Between 2 A10 and 4 A10 GPUs

When examining vLLM's performance across 2 vs 4 GPUs, we can observe:

1. For 30 concurrent requests

   :

   - 2 GPUs: 11.12-12.00 req/s, ~1217 tokens/s, avg response time ~2.09s
   - 4 GPUs: 13.12-13.22 req/s, ~1319 tokens/s, avg response time ~1.90s

2. For 50 concurrent requests

   :

   - 2 GPUs: 15.07-15.27 req/s, ~1527 tokens/s, avg response time ~2.72s
   - 4 GPUs: 15.20-15.61 req/s, ~1550 tokens/s, avg response time ~2.70s

3. For 100 concurrent requests

   :

   - 2 GPUs: 17.40-17.99 req/s, ~1787 tokens/s, avg response time ~4.63s
   - 4 GPUs: 18.73-19.13 req/s, ~1901 tokens/s, avg response time ~4.43s

The conclusion for vLLM is that it does show some scaling improvements when moving from 2 to 4 GPUs, but the gains are modest:

- Throughput improves by approximately 15-20% for 30 concurrent requests
- Throughput improves by only 2-5% for 50 concurrent requests
- Throughput improves by approximately 6-10% for 100 concurrent requests

This suggests that vLLM's scaling efficiency decreases as the number of GPUs increases, with diminishing returns at higher GPU counts. The model may be hitting bottlenecks that aren't related to raw compute capacity (potentially communication overhead between GPUs or other system limitations).

In contrast, SGLang with tensor parallelism shows more dramatic scaling improvements when moving from 2 to 4 GPUs:

- For 30 concurrent requests: 10.78 req/s → 20.27 req/s (88% improvement)
- For 50 concurrent requests: 17.52 req/s → 24.48 req/s (40% improvement)

This is a significant finding that could be highlighted in your conclusion - SGLang appears to scale more efficiently with additional GPUs than vLLM for this specific workload and model.
